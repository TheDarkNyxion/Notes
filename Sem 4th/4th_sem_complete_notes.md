# PART 1: DIGITAL & CYBER FORENSICS

## UNIT I: CYBER CRIMES

### Core Concepts

**Cyber Forensics and Computer Crimes**
- Cyber forensics is the application of investigative and forensic techniques to uncover evidence in digital devices
- Bridges the gap between traditional law enforcement and the digital realm
- Involves the collection, preservation, and analysis of digital evidence

**Conventional vs. Cyber Crime**
- Conventional crimes: Physical evidence, visible perpetrators, traditional investigation methods
- Cyber crimes: Digital footprints, anonymous perpetrators, requires technical expertise, leaves audit trails in logs
- Key difference: Location independence - cyber crimes can be committed globally instantaneously

**Reasons for Cyber Crime**
- Financial gain (theft, fraud, extortion)
- Espionage and competitive intelligence
- Activism and ideological motivations
- Revenge and personal grudges
- Intellectual property theft
- Power and notoriety
- System vulnerabilities exploitation

**Types of Cyber Crimes**

1. **Crimes Targeting Computers**
   - Unauthorized access (hacking, intrusion)
   - Installation of malicious code
   - Data theft and espionage
   - Denial of Service (DoS) attacks
   - System manipulation and sabotage

2. **Network-Based Crimes**
   - Packet sniffing: Interception of network packets to capture sensitive data
   - Man-in-the-middle attacks: Positioning between two communicating parties
   - IP spoofing: Forging IP addresses to appear as trusted source
   - DNS spoofing: Redirecting traffic to attacker-controlled servers

3. **Malicious Code**
   - Trojans: Disguised legitimate software containing malicious payload
   - Viruses: Self-replicating code attached to legitimate programs
   - Worms: Self-propagating code that exploits vulnerabilities
   - Logic bombs: Dormant malicious code triggered by specific conditions
   - Ransomware: Encryption-based extortion
   - Spyware: Covert surveillance and data harvesting

4. **Phishing and Variants**
   - Email phishing: Fraudulent emails requesting credentials
   - Spear phishing: Targeted phishing with personalized information
   - Whaling: Targeting high-value executives
   - Vishing: Voice-based phishing
   - Smishing: SMS-based phishing
   - Clone phishing: Creating replica of legitimate messages

5. **Spoofing and Its Types**
   - Email spoofing: Falsifying sender identity
   - Website spoofing: Creating fake versions of legitimate sites
   - Domain spoofing: Registering similar domain names
   - ARP spoofing: Mapping fake MAC addresses to IP addresses

6. **Cyber Stalking**
   - Harassment through repeated digital contact
   - Monitoring and surveillance via digital means
   - Threats and intimidation online
   - Location tracking and doxing

7. **Web Defacement**
   - Unauthorized modification of website content
   - Insertion of malicious content
   - Display of attacker's message
   - Damage to organizational reputation

8. **Financial Crimes**
   - Online banking fraud
   - Credit card fraud and identity theft
   - Investment scams
   - Cryptocurrency theft
   - ATM skimming and digital manipulation

9. **Intellectual Property Crimes**
   - Software piracy: Unauthorized copying and distribution
   - Hardware piracy: Counterfeit components
   - Cheating: Academic and competitive dishonesty
   - Trade secret theft

10. **Hacking and Its Types**
    - White hat: Ethical hacking with permission
    - Black hat: Malicious hacking for personal gain
    - Grey hat: Hacking without explicit permission but without malicious intent
    - Script kiddies: Using pre-made tools without deep knowledge
    - Hacktivism: Hacking for political/social causes

**Prevention Measures**
- Multi-factor authentication (MFA) implementation
- Regular security awareness training
- Encryption of sensitive data (both in transit and at rest)
- Regular software patching and updates
- Network segmentation and firewalls
- Intrusion detection systems (IDS) and intrusion prevention systems (IPS)
- Regular security audits and penetration testing
- Incident response planning
- Backup and recovery procedures
- Zero-trust architecture implementation

## UNIT II: DIGITAL EVIDENCE

### Core Concepts

**Definition and Introduction**
- Digital evidence: Information and data of investigative value stored on or transmitted through digital devices
- Can be primary (created digitally) or secondary (by-product of digital processes)
- Must maintain integrity, authenticity, and chain of custody

**Types of Digital Evidence**

1. **By Source**
   - Computer systems (desktops, laptops, servers)
   - Mobile devices (smartphones, tablets)
   - Storage media (hard drives, SSDs, USB drives, SD cards)
   - Network devices (routers, switches, firewalls)
   - Cloud-based data
   - IoT devices (smartwatches, cameras, thermostats)

2. **By Volatility**
   - Volatile: Data that exists in RAM, logs, cache; lost when power is removed
   - Non-volatile: Data stored on persistent media; survives shutdown

3. **By Media Type**
   - Hard disks (mechanical and SSD)
   - Smart cards and SIM cards
   - Optical media (CD, DVD, Blu-ray)
   - Magnetic media (older systems)
   - Removable storage (USB, memory cards)
   - Virtual storage and cloud systems

**Admissibility of Digital Evidence in Court**

Requirements for admissibility:
- **Relevance**: Evidence must relate to the case and have probative value
- **Authenticity**: Must be proven to be original and unaltered
- **Reliability**: Chain of custody must be maintained throughout
- **Integrity**: No tampering, modification, or corruption
- **Competence**: Evidence must be acquired using proper procedures
- **Compliance**: Must follow legal and constitutional standards

Indian legal framework:
- Indian Evidence Act, 1872: Sections 61-65 address electronic records
- Information Technology Act, 2000: Sections 65A and 65B for admissibility of electronic evidence
- Requirements: Proper authentication, metadata preservation, examiner qualifications

**Nature of Digital Evidence**

- **Fragile**: Easily corrupted or destroyed
- **Complex**: Requires specialized knowledge to interpret
- **Voluminous**: Can contain enormous amounts of data
- **Temporal**: Contains timestamps and sequence information
- **Transient**: Some data exists only during execution
- **Invisible**: User-invisible metadata and artifacts
- **Reconstructible**: Can be recovered even after deletion

**Volatile and Non-Volatile Evidence**

Volatile Evidence Collection Priority:
- System memory (RAM) contents
- Running processes
- Network connections
- Open files
- Logged-in users
- System date/time
- Command history
- Temporary files in memory

Non-Volatile Evidence:
- Hard drive contents
- File systems
- Deleted files
- Hidden partitions
- Master boot records
- Slack space
- Swap files

**Digital Evidence Collection and Transportation**

Collection procedures:
1. Preliminary assessment and documentation
2. Photograph evidence in situ
3. Identify and document connections
4. Ensure proper power state (don't force shutdown if running)
5. Use forensic tools and write-blockers
6. Document all steps with timestamps
7. Create bit-level copies/images
8. Calculate cryptographic hashes (MD5, SHA-256)
9. Seal and document chain of custody
10. Label all media with case information

Transportation guidelines:
- Use anti-static bags for storage media
- Maintain temperature and humidity control
- Protect from magnetic fields
- Use tamper-evident seals
- Document condition at every transfer
- Maintain chain of custody documentation
- Store in secure, climate-controlled facility

**Characteristics of Digital Evidence Types**

Hard Disks:
- Storage capacity and speed vary
- Contain file systems (FAT, NTFS, ext4, etc.)
- Contain deleted files in unallocated space
- Sector and cluster structures
- Master boot record and partition tables
- Risk of physical damage during acquisition

Smart Cards:
- Limited storage (typically 64KB to 256KB)
- Encrypted data storage
- PIN protection
- Application-specific data
- Difficult to acquire without damaging

Handheld Devices (PDAs, Smartphones):
- Operating systems (iOS, Android, Windows Mobile)
- SIM card data
- App-specific storage
- Cloud synchronization
- Encrypted storage
- Volatile RAM contents
- Battery-dependent operation

Removable Storage Media:
- USB drives: Flash memory, variable speed
- Memory cards: SD, microSD, CF cards
- External hard drives: Similar to internal drives
- Optical discs: CD, DVD, Blu-ray
- Magnetic tape: Older backup media
- Variable capacities and formats

SIM Cards:
- Contains IMSI (International Mobile Subscriber Identity)
- Phone numbers and contacts
- SMS messages
- Service provider information
- Authentication keys
- Limited storage

Network Devices (Servers, Routers, Hubs, Switches):
- Configuration files
- Log files and audit trails
- Network traffic logs
- DHCP lease information
- ARP tables
- Routing tables
- Connection logs

**Precautions While Dealing with Digital Evidence**

Physical precautions:
- Use anti-static wrist straps and mats
- Avoid electrostatic discharge
- Protect from physical damage
- Prevent contamination
- Control temperature and humidity
- Shield from magnetic fields
- Use clean tools and equipment

Technical precautions:
- Write-block all evidence before analysis
- Document original state before acquisition
- Create forensic-quality bit-level copies
- Maintain chain of custody
- Use validated tools and procedures
- Document all analysis steps
- Calculate and verify cryptographic hashes
- Preserve metadata
- Avoid accessing evidence directly
- Use separate isolated analysis systems

**Slack Space**
- Unallocated space within a cluster after file ends
- Contains fragments of deleted files
- Important evidence source
- Can contain previous file remnants
- Requires specialized tools to examine
- File system dependent

**Swap Space**
- Virtual memory allocated on disk
- Contains paged-out memory contents
- User session data
- Encryption keys and credentials
- Passwords and sensitive information
- Volatile but recoverable
- Present on all modern systems

## UNIT III: COMPUTER FORENSICS

### Core Concepts

**Types of Storage Media**

Primary Storage:

RAM (Random Access Memory):
- Volatile memory: Lost when power is removed
- Measured in GB (typical modern systems have 4-32 GB)
- DRAM (Dynamic RAM): Requires constant refresh
- SRAM (Static RAM): Faster but more expensive
- Stores running processes, encryption keys, credentials
- Contains network connections, open files, command history

Types of RAM:
- SDR (Single Data Rate): Older technology
- DDR (Double Data Rate): DDR, DDR2, DDR3, DDR4, DDR5
- Bandwidth increases with each generation
- Capacity limitations vary by processor and motherboard

ROM (Read-Only Memory):
- Non-volatile: Data persists when powered off
- Contains firmware and boot code
- Cannot be modified during normal operation
- BIOS/UEFI stored in ROM
- Immutable until factory reset
- Limited forensic value in most cases

Types of ROM:
- PROM (Programmable ROM): One-time programmable
- EPROM (Erasable PROM): Erasable with UV light
- EEPROM (Electrically Erasable PROM): Electrically erasable
- Flash ROM: Modern implementation

Secondary Storage:

Magnetic Tape:
- Sequential access (slower for random access)
- Excellent for archival storage (decades of lifespan)
- High capacity (multiple terabytes per cartridge)
- Used for backup systems
- Forensic challenges: Sequential nature, large volumes
- Vulnerable to magnetic fields

Magnetic Disk:
- Rotational storage (platters rotating at 5400-15000 RPM)
- Traditional hard disk drives (HDDs)
- Random access (faster than tape)
- Sectors and tracks for data organization
- Head positioning time (latency)
- Subject to mechanical failure
- Recoverable even after deletion

Optical Disk:
- CD (650 MB - 700 MB): Read-only or writable
- DVD (4.7 GB - 8.5 GB): Higher density than CD
- Blu-ray (25 GB - 50 GB): Even higher density
- WORM (Write-Once Read-Many) media
- Pit and land structure for data encoding
- Resistant to magnetic fields
- Limited rewrite cycles for writable media

Solid State Drive (SSD):
- Flash memory based (no moving parts)
- Much faster than mechanical drives
- Lower power consumption
- Wear-leveling algorithms
- TRIM command for cell management
- Forensic challenges: Rapid data overwriting, encryption

**Importance in Digital Forensics**

- Different media requires different acquisition methods
- Storage type affects recovery possibilities
- Media understanding enables targeted investigation
- Recovery difficulty varies by storage type
- Magnetic media easier to recover than SSD
- Proper handling prevents data loss

**Hard Disk Structure**

Physical Components:
- Platters: Rotating magnetic media (aluminum, glass, or ceramic)
- Actuator arm: Positions read/write heads
- Read/write heads: Magnetic sensors reading/writing data
- Motor: Spins platters at constant speed
- Controller: Manages all disk operations
- Power supply: Provides power to all components

Logical Structure:
- Cylinder: Same track across all platters
- Track: Concentric ring on platter surface
- Sector: Smallest addressable unit (typically 512 bytes or 4KB)
- Cluster: Multiple sectors grouped together
- Partition: Logical division of disk space
- Volume: Formatted partition ready for data storage

**FAT File System**

FAT12, FAT16, FAT32:
- File Allocation Table: Table mapping clusters to files
- Simple structure: Easy to understand and analyze
- Limited file size (max 2GB for FAT16, 4GB for FAT32)
- Flat directory structure (FAT12/16) or hierarchical (FAT32)
- No access control or file-level encryption
- Fragmentation common (files split across clusters)
- Deleted files: FAT entries deleted but data remains
- Recovery: Search for file signatures and reconstruct

FAT Structure:
- Boot sector: File system parameters and statistics
- FAT table: Entries for each cluster (may be duplicated)
- Root directory: Initial directory entries
- Data area: Actual file content

**NTFS File System**

New Technology File System:
- Modern Microsoft file system (Windows NT onwards)
- Journaling: Transaction log for crash recovery
- Maximum file size: 16 exabytes (theoretical)
- Hierarchical directory structure
- Access control lists (ACLs)
- Encryption support (EFS)
- Alternate data streams (ADS): Hidden data in files
- Master File Table (MFT): All file metadata

NTFS Structures:
- Boot sector: File system parameters
- MFT: Primary file metadata storage
- MFT mirror: Backup copy of first 4 records
- Cluster allocation bitmap: Shows allocated/free clusters
- Alternate data streams: Extended attributes
- Journal/log file: Transaction record
- Bad cluster file: Damaged sector tracking

Forensic implications:
- MFT contains file timestamps (created, modified, accessed)
- Deleted files: Entry marked deleted but data may remain
- Slack space: Room for file remnants
- MFT slack: Unused space in MFT records
- Alternate data streams: May contain hidden evidence

**Sources of Digital Evidence in Hard Disk**

File system artifacts:
- Active files: Current user data
- Deleted files: Recoverable from unallocated space
- File metadata: Timestamps, permissions, size
- File names: Original naming conventions
- Directory structure: Organizational hierarchy
- Unallocated clusters: Space not assigned to files
- Bad clusters: Damaged but possibly containing data

System artifacts:
- Event logs: Security, system, application events
- Registry: Windows configuration and user data
- Pagefile: Virtual memory contents
- Hibernation file: Memory dump during sleep
- Prefetch: Program execution history
- Recent files list: User activity tracking
- Temporary files: Application caches

**Partition**

Logical division of disk space:
- Master Boot Record (MBR): Legacy partition table
- GUID Partition Table (GPT): Modern partition scheme
- Extended partitions: Additional logical partitions
- Partition table: Maps partitions to disk sectors
- Boot sector: File system parameters
- Multiple file systems possible on single drive
- Hidden partitions: Not typically visible to OS

**Pen Drive (USB Flash Drive)**

Characteristics:
- Flash memory storage
- Limited write cycles (wear-out)
- Encryption capability
- No mechanical components
- Fast data transfer
- Typically 8GB to 1TB capacity
- File systems: FAT32, NTFS, ext4

Forensic considerations:
- TRIM command speeds up overwriting
- Wear-leveling distributes writes
- Rapid data overwriting
- Possible recovery from NAND chips directly
- Metadata less reliable than hard drives

**Mobile Device Storage**

Android/Linux-based systems:
- Flash-based internal storage
- SD card support (expandable)
- Partition structure: system, data, cache
- ext4 or F2FS file system
- Wear-leveling and TRIM support
- Data encryption possible

iOS systems:
- NAND flash storage
- No expandable storage
- Proprietary file system
- Encrypted by default
- Secure enclave for encryption keys
- Limited user access to file system

Forensic challenges:
- Encryption by default
- Frequent automatic backups
- Cloud synchronization
- Limited direct file system access
- Fast data overwriting
- Device-specific tools needed

## UNIT IV: INCIDENT RESPONSE

### Core Concepts

**Introduction to Incident Response Process**

Definition:
- Structured methodology for detecting, responding to, and managing security breaches
- Minimizes impact, reduces recovery time, and prevents future incidents
- Involves technical, administrative, and management personnel

Incident Response Team:
- Security manager/director: Overall coordination
- Security analysts: Threat assessment and analysis
- Network administrators: System access and logs
- System administrators: Evidence preservation
- Legal counsel: Regulatory compliance
- Communications: Internal/external messaging
- Management: Resource allocation and decision-making

**Role of First Responder**

Responsibilities:
- Arrive at scene and verify incident occurrence
- Document initial state of systems and environment
- Preserve evidence and prevent tampering
- Secure the affected area
- Identify involved systems and users
- Collect initial evidence with minimal disturbance
- Document chain of custody
- Report findings to incident commander
- Prevent escalation and spread of incident

Skills required:
- Technical knowledge of systems and networks
- Evidence preservation understanding
- Communication and documentation abilities
- Calm decision-making under pressure
- Legal and privacy awareness
- Photography and note-taking skills

**Common First Responder Mistakes**

Technical mistakes:
- Connecting suspect devices to network (introduces malware)
- Powering on/off systems improperly (data loss)
- Installing software on evidence systems (contamination)
- Plugging evidence into non-forensic systems
- Using non-validated tools
- Overwriting evidence through normal operations
- Failing to preserve volatile memory data
- Making unauthorized changes to system configuration

Procedural mistakes:
- Inadequate documentation of initial conditions
- Poor chain of custody maintenance
- Failure to photograph evidence in place
- Mixing evidence from different cases
- Discussing case details publicly
- Contaminating evidence through improper handling
- Failing to note date/time of observations
- Using personal devices for documentation

**Computer Security Incident Definition**

Characteristics:
- Unauthorized access to systems
- Unauthorized modification of data or systems
- Denial of service impacting legitimate users
- Violation of security policy
- Exploitation of vulnerabilities
- Malware infection
- Data exfiltration
- Breach of confidentiality, integrity, or availability

Severity levels:
- Critical: Complete system compromise, large-scale data loss
- High: Significant access, sensitive data threatened
- Medium: Limited access, isolated systems affected
- Low: Unsuccessful attacks, minimal impact

**Goals of Incident Response**

1. **Detection and Analysis**
   - Identify that incident occurred
   - Classify severity and type
   - Determine scope of compromise
   - Identify affected systems and data

2. **Containment**
   - Stop spread of incident
   - Isolate affected systems
   - Prevent additional damage
   - Preserve evidence for investigation

3. **Eradication**
   - Remove malware and attacker access
   - Patch vulnerabilities
   - Restore systems to known-good state
   - Remove unauthorized access methods

4. **Recovery**
   - Restore systems to normal operation
   - Monitor for re-compromise
   - Implement stronger controls
   - Restore data from backups
   - Resume normal business operations

5. **Post-Incident Activities**
   - Conduct forensic analysis
   - Document lessons learned
   - Improve detection capabilities
   - Update incident response procedures
   - Share threat intelligence
   - Implement preventive measures

**Incident Response Methodology**

NIST SP 800-61 Four-Phase Model:

1. **Preparation**
   - Tools and resources: Forensic software, hardware, documentation templates
   - Training: Regular drills and exercises
   - Communication: Escalation procedures, contact lists
   - Policy: Incident response policy and procedures
   - Detection capability: IDS/IPS, SIEM systems

2. **Detection and Analysis**
   - Alert triage: Determine if incident occurred
   - Preliminary investigation: Initial assessment
   - Incident classification: Type and severity
   - Initial containment: Prevent spread
   - Notification: Alert stakeholders
   - Initial documentation: Timeline and evidence

3. **Containment, Eradication, Recovery**
   - Short-term containment: Rapid response
   - Long-term containment: Complete removal
   - Eradication: Complete compromise removal
   - Recovery: System restoration
   - Verification: Confirmation of restoration

4. **Post-Incident Activity**
   - Lessons learned meeting: Team discussion
   - Report generation: Formal documentation
   - Evidence retention: Archival for legal/forensic needs
   - Process improvement: Procedure updates
   - Metrics and reporting: Performance analysis

**Incident Response Roles and Involvement**

Incident Commander:
- Overall coordination and decision-making
- Resource allocation
- Stakeholder communication
- Priority determination

Technical Team:
- System and network analysis
- Evidence collection
- Malware analysis
- Vulnerability assessment
- System recovery

Management:
- Business impact assessment
- Resource approval
- Decision authority
- Regulatory reporting

Legal/Compliance:
- Evidence handling requirements
- Notification obligations
- Privilege protection
- Regulatory compliance

Communications:
- Internal communications
- External statements
- Customer notification
- Media management

**Formulate a Response Strategy**

Assessment phase:
- Determine incident scope and severity
- Identify affected systems, data, and users
- Assess business impact
- Estimate recovery time
- Identify available resources
- Review prior incidents for patterns

Strategy options:

Immediate Containment (High-Risk):
- Isolate affected systems immediately
- Preserve evidence
- Rapid eradication
- Suitable for: Active attacks, data exfiltration, ransomware

Phased Approach (Medium-Risk):
- Monitor attacker activity
- Gather additional evidence
- Coordinate larger response
- Suitable for: Persistent threats, advanced attacks

Investigation-First (Low-Risk):
- Detailed forensic analysis
- Understand full scope
- Plan comprehensive response
- Suitable for: Completed attacks, historical compromise

**Investigate the Incident**

Investigation components:

Timeline reconstruction:
- Identify earliest evidence of compromise
- Map progression of attack
- Identify activity patterns
- Determine attack methods
- Document actions taken

Evidence collection:
- System logs and event logs
- Network traffic captures (PCAP)
- File system artifacts
- Memory dumps
- Configuration files
- User activity records

Forensic analysis:
- File deletion recovery
- Hidden file and alternate data stream discovery
- User account analysis
- Service and process analysis
- Persistence mechanism identification
- Command history examination
- Network connection analysis

Threat actor identification:
- Attack methodology analysis
- Tool usage patterns
- Malware characteristics
- Targeting patterns
- Infrastructure indicators
- Known threat actor profile matching

**Preparing for Incident Response**

Pre-incident preparation:

Inventory:
- System and network documentation
- Asset management database
- Software inventory
- Configuration baselines
- Data classification scheme

Tools and resources:
- Forensic acquisition tools
- Analysis software
- Hardware (write-blockers, etc.)
- Isolation equipment
- Communication devices
- Response playbooks

Personnel:
- Team training and certification
- Regular drills and tabletop exercises
- Clear role assignments
- Contact information
- Escalation procedures
- Cross-training

Policies and procedures:
- Incident response plan
- Communication procedures
- Evidence handling procedures
- Retention policies
- Backup and recovery procedures
- Business continuity plans

Threat intelligence:
- Known threat actor profiles
- Vulnerability awareness
- Exploit availability monitoring
- Baseline threat landscape
- Industry-specific threats
- Current threat feeds

**Identifying Risk**

Risk assessment:
- Critical asset identification
- Threat likelihood assessment
- Impact determination
- Vulnerability analysis
- Risk rating calculation
- Control evaluation

Risk factors:
- System exposure (Internet-facing, internal only)
- Software vulnerabilities
- Misconfigurations
- Access control weaknesses
- Lack of monitoring
- Outdated systems
- User behavior risks
- Third-party risks

**After Detection of an Incident**

Immediate actions (0-1 hour):
- Confirm incident occurred
- Engage incident response team
- Establish command center
- Begin initial documentation
- Alert management and legal
- Start evidence preservation

Short-term actions (1-24 hours):
- Detailed investigation initiation
- Containment measures implementation
- Forensic imaging of affected systems
- Network traffic analysis
- Log analysis
- Preliminary impact assessment
- Stakeholder notification

Medium-term actions (1-7 days):
- Full forensic analysis
- Vulnerability remediation
- System restoration planning
- Additional system hardening
- Threat actor research
- Process improvement identification

Long-term actions (ongoing):
- Lessons learned documentation
- Policy and procedure updates
- Control improvements
- Detection capability enhancement
- Team training
- Regular response drills

# PART 2: QUESTIONED DOCUMENTS

## UNIT I: INTRODUCTION TO QUESTIONED DOCUMENTS

### Core Concepts

**Nature and Scope of Questioned Documents**

Definition:
- Any written, printed, or electronic document whose authenticity, origin, content, or authorship is in question
- Includes both physical and digital documents
- Can be evidence in civil, criminal, and administrative proceedings

Scope:
- Handwriting analysis and comparison
- Signature examination
- Typewriter and printer identification
- Alteration and forgery detection
- Age determination
- Authorship identification
- Document authenticity assessment
- Ink and paper analysis
- Printing technology identification

Types of questioned documents:
- **Signatures**: Fraudulent vs. genuine
- **Letters and notes**: Anonymous vs. known authorship
- **Checks and financial instruments**: Forgery, alterations
- **Identification documents**: Counterfeit or altered
- **Medical records**: Alteration, backdating
- **Legal documents**: Forgery, unauthorized modifications
- **Currency and stamps**: Counterfeit
- **Typed documents**: Authorship and printer identification
- **Printed materials**: Authenticity and source

**Definition of Questioned Documents**

Formal definition:
- Document in which one or more of the following elements are disputed:
  - Origin (who created it)
  - Authenticity (is it real)
  - Content (what it says)
  - Authorization (was it authorized by the signatory)
  - Age (when was it created)
  - Completeness (was it altered)
  - Method of production
  - Authorship

Key characteristics:
- Created or modified with intent to deceive
- Disputed by one or more parties
- May contain evidence of crime or misconduct
- Requires expert examination
- Can affect legal decisions

**Types of Documents / Classification of Documents**

By content type:
- Correspondence: Letters, memos, emails
- Financial: Checks, promissory notes, invoices
- Legal: Wills, contracts, agreements, deeds
- Identification: Passports, driver's licenses, birth certificates
- Medical: Prescriptions, medical records, death certificates
- Currency: Banknotes, coins
- Administrative: Records, forms, permits

By creation method:
- Handwritten: Personal creation
- Typewritten: Machine creation (typewriter)
- Printed: Mass-produced
- Electronically created: Computer/digital devices
- Photocopied: Reproductions
- Hybrid: Combination of methods

By function:
- Original: First/primary version
- Copy: Reproduction
- Duplicate: Certified equivalent
- Facsimile: Fax transmission
- Scan: Digital reproduction

By authenticity status:
- Genuine: As represented
- Spurious: Not as represented
- Forged: Intentionally fraudulent
- Altered: Modified after creation
- Simulated: Imitation of genuine

**Writing Instruments and Their Characteristics**

Ballpoint pens:
- Mechanism: Rotating ball distributes ink
- Ink composition: Oil-based, non-soluble
- Pressure sensitivity: Moderate variation
- Line characteristics: Consistent width
- Ink color: Wide range available
- Aging: Stable over time
- Identification: Pen type, manufacturer
- Evidence value: Ink matching possible

Gel pens:
- Mechanism: Porous ball tip
- Ink composition: Water-based, soluble
- Pressure sensitivity: More sensitive than ballpoint
- Line characteristics: Variable width
- Ink color: Vibrant colors available
- Aging: Can fade
- Identification: Brand-specific characteristics
- Evidence value: Ink matching possible

Fountain pens:
- Mechanism: Nib and ink feed system
- Ink composition: Water-based, soluble
- Pressure sensitivity: High variation
- Line characteristics: Thick-thin variation
- Ink color: Traditional colors
- Aging: Ink fades
- Identification: Nib shape and size
- Evidence value: Handwriting characteristics

Markers and highlighters:
- Mechanism: Felt tip application
- Ink composition: Oil or water-based
- Pressure sensitivity: Low sensitivity
- Line characteristics: Thick strokes
- Ink color: Limited range, bright
- Aging: Fading possible
- Identification: Tip size and shape
- Evidence value: Less suitable for signature analysis

Pencils:
- Mechanism: Graphite core with pressure
- "Ink" composition: Graphite - erasable
- Pressure sensitivity: Very high
- Line characteristics: Variable darkness
- "Ink" color: Gray to black
- Aging: Unstable, can fade
- Identification: Lead hardness (H, HB, B grades)
- Evidence value: Easily erasable, not suitable for authentic signatures

Digital input devices:
- Stylus on tablets
- Touch screens
- Digital signatures
- Pressure sensitivity: High in professional devices
- Line characteristics: Accurate reproduction
- Identification: Device and software specific
- Evidence value: Metadata containing creation information

**Ink and Their Examination**

Ink composition:
- Dyes or pigments (coloring agents)
- Solvents (dispersal medium)
- Resins (adhesion)
- Lubricants (flow)
- Stabilizers (preservation)

Ink characteristics:
- Color: Varies widely
- Viscosity: Affects flow
- Drying time: Hours to days
- Solubility: Response to solvents
- Age: Changes over time
- Manufacturer: Batch variations

Ink examination techniques:
- Visual examination: Under magnification
- UV/fluorescence: Detection of invisible ink
- Infrared: Penetration of surface
- Thin layer chromatography (TLC): Separation of components
- Mass spectrometry: Molecular composition
- X-ray fluorescence (XRF): Elemental analysis
- Comparison: Known vs. questioned samples

**Paper and Its Examination**

Paper composition:
- Cellulose fibers: Primary component
- Fillers: Calcium carbonate, clay (brightness, opacity)
- Sizing agents: Starch, gum arabic (water resistance)
- Colorants: Dyes or pigments
- Additives: For specific properties

Paper characteristics:
- Weight: Thickness and density
- Brightness: Reflectance percentage
- Opacity: Light transmission
- Texture: Smooth vs. rough surface
- Finish: Glossy, matte, satin
- Watermark: Embedded design
- Grain direction: Fiber orientation
- Chain lines: Visible pattern from manufacturing

Paper examination techniques:
- Visual inspection: Color, texture, markings
- Magnification: Fiber analysis
- Microscopy: Fiber identification
- UV examination: Brightness and content
- Fluorescence: Additives and brighteners
- Infrared: Component analysis
- Watermark viewing: Transmitted light
- Fiber analysis: Microscopic identification
- Chemical tests: Composition determination
- FTIR (Fourier Transform Infrared): Spectral analysis

**Nature of Document Examination**

Examination process:
- Non-destructive when possible
- Systematic and methodical
- Documented with photographs and notes
- Compared to known standards
- Multiple techniques applied
- Qualified expert required
- Objective findings recorded
- Conclusions stated with confidence levels

Examination goals:
- Establish document authenticity
- Identify alterations or modifications
- Determine authorship
- Ascertain document age
- Identify document source
- Detect forged signatures
- Determine writing instrument
- Identify printing method

Methodology:
- Preliminary examination: Initial overview
- Detailed examination: Systematic analysis
- Comparative analysis: Questioned vs. known
- Specialized testing: As needed
- Documentation: Photographs, notes, findings
- Report preparation: Expert conclusions
- Expert testimony: Court presentation if needed
